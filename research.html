“Is Rich Phenomenology Fragmented?”

Some philosophers argue that the content of iconic memory is conscious, called the Rich View. However, critics maintain that only fragments of the content of iconic memory are conscious, called the Fragment View. Both sides cite different psychological experimental data to support their positions. Proponents of the Fragment View tend to assert that their view uniquely explains the data they rely on. The uniqueness of the Fragment View is challenged here. Newly introduced evidence suggests that the data supporting the Fragment View may also be compatible with the Rich View. Given the theoretical advantages of the Rich View in other respects, there are reasons to consider it the superior one. [Read]

“Do Semantic Properties Involve the Future?”

The meaning of linguistic expressions is closely connected to external facts. While standard externalism links meaning to past and present facts, the more recent view of Temporal Externalism holds that future facts can also determine meaning. This paper argues that such a view is difficult to sustain. I develop four main arguments. First, I propose a moderate constraint principle for detecting semantic shift, which involves changes in metalinguistic beliefs and linguistic practice. Second, I introduce a distinction between metalinguistic beliefs and quasi-metalinguistic beliefs, and argue that the latter can also satisfy the proposed principle. Third, I argue that Temporal Externalism misclassifies these quasi-metalinguistic beliefs as object-level beliefs. Once we take this point seriously, we find that the reference of key terms actually shifts—contrary to what the theory itself claims. Finally, I examine two possible responses: appealing to a kind of meta-commitment, or adopting a version of Revisionary Temporal Externalism. I argue that both responses place the theory in a dilemma.
